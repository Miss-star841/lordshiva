Mount Kailash

Mount Kailash, a majestic peak in the Himalayas, holds profound religious significance for four major faiths: Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, and the Tibetan Bon religion. Rising to 6,638 meters (21,778 feet) in the remote southwestern corner of Tibet, it's revered as a sacred mountain, a place of pilgrimage, and a source of major Asian rivers. Despite its prominence, Mount Kailash has never been climbed, adding to its mystique and spiritual aura.

Geographical and Geological Features:

Mount Kailash is a striking, diamond-shaped peak with four distinct faces, each oriented towards a cardinal direction. The area is also the source of four major rivers: the Indus, Sutlej, Brahmaputra, and Karnali.

Spiritual Significance:

For Hindus, Mount Kailash is the abode of Lord Shiva and his consort Parvati. Buddhists revere it as the center of the universe and the home of Demchog (also known as Chakrasamvara). Jains consider it the place where the first Jain Tirthankara, Rishabhadeva, attained liberation. The Bon religion, indigenous to Tibet, also holds Mount Kailash sacred, believing it to be the "Nine-Story Swastika Mountain". Pilgrims undertake a ritual circumambulation (kora) of the mountain, a 52-kilometer trek believed to bring good fortune and cleanse sins.
